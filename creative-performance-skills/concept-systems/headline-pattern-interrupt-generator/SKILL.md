# Headline Pattern Interrupt Generator

**Category:** concept-systems
**Tier:** pro

## Purpose

This Skill generates scroll-stopping headlines using pattern interrupt mechanics, specificity, numbers, and direct-response structure. A headline in paid social has one job: make the next word inevitable. Pattern interrupts work by violating the audience's expectations of what an ad looks like or says. Specificity works because specific claims are inherently more credible and interesting than vague ones. This Skill combines both.

---

## When to Use

- You need headline variations for A/B testing (hook testing)
- Your current ads have low CTR and you suspect the headline is the weak point
- You're building a concept library and need headlines across multiple formats and angles
- You want to generate 20-30 headline options quickly to identify the best candidates for testing
- You're briefing a creative team and need headline examples to set the standard
- You want to test different pattern interrupt types against each other

---

## Inputs Required

- Product or service and its primary benefit
- Target audience (who they are, what they want, what they're afraid of)
- Key number or specific outcome available (results, timeline, price, quantity)
- Awareness stage targeted
- Top objection to address (optional)
- Emotional angle: curiosity, fear, aspiration, frustration, skepticism, surprise
- Existing headlines that have performed well or poorly (optional)

---

## What This Skill Does

Generates 25-40 headlines across 8 pattern interrupt categories, using specific numbers, direct-address, and format-specific mechanics. Each headline is labeled with its pattern interrupt type, awareness stage match, and a brief note on the emotion it activates.

---

## Analysis Workflow

1. **Extract the specificity elements**
   From the provided inputs, extract every number, measurement, timeframe, outcome, price, count, or percentage available. These are the raw material for specific headlines.

2. **Identify the primary audience tension**
   What is the dominant emotional state of the audience at this awareness stage? Fear, frustration, aspiration, skepticism? Headlines must enter from the audience's emotional reality, not from the brand's desired message.

3. **Apply the 8 Pattern Interrupt Types**

   **Type 1 — The Specific Number:** Uses a real, specific number that creates credibility and curiosity.
   "Lost 18.4 lbs in 31 days without the gym."
   "3,847 freelancers use this system to reclaim 7 hours per week."

   **Type 2 — The Counter-Intuitive Claim:** Says the opposite of what the audience expects to hear.
   "Stop trying to wake up at 5am." / "The worst thing you can do for your skin is moisturize."

   **Type 3 — The Specificity Contrast:** Contrasts the vague problem with a hyper-specific solution.
   "Tired of 'eat healthy'? Here's the exact 4-meal system I used to drop 2 pants sizes."

   **Type 4 — The Direct Address:** Names the audience by their specific identity, role, or situation.
   "For freelancers charging $50/hr who should be charging $150." / "If you haven't slept more than 5 hours in the last 3 nights."

   **Type 5 — The Unexpected Failure Reveal:** Suggests the audience is making a mistake they didn't know they were making.
   "The reason your Facebook ads aren't converting (it's not the creative)."

   **Type 6 — The Time-Specific Claim:** Grounds the outcome in a specific, believable timeframe.
   "My first paying client came from this strategy in 11 days." / "4 minutes per day. $2,300 in 6 weeks."

   **Type 7 — The Identity Challenger:** Challenges or reframes the audience's self-concept.
   "You're not bad at saving money. You're using the wrong system." / "If you're calling yourself a media buyer, you need to see this."

   **Type 8 — The Dramatic Proof Drop:** Opens with proof so specific it reads as unbelievable — but is.
   "She paid off $41,000 in credit card debt while earning $38,000/yr." / "This 4-line email got a 94% open rate."

4. **Generate headlines in each category**
   Produce 3-5 headlines per pattern interrupt type, totaling 25-40 headlines.

5. **Add awareness stage tags**
   Label each headline: which awareness stage it speaks to. A Direct Address headline aimed at people who "have tried everything" is Problem Aware. An offer-specific headline is Most Aware.

6. **Flag the top 5 for immediate testing**
   Identify the 5 headlines with the strongest combination of: specificity, pattern interrupt strength, awareness stage fit, and emotional charge.

---

## Output Requirements

**Headlines by Pattern Interrupt Type**
Each type labeled as a section with 3-5 headlines, each with:
- Headline text
- Pattern interrupt type
- Awareness stage
- Emotion activated (frustration / aspiration / fear / curiosity / skepticism)

**Top 5 Test Recommendations**
The 5 headlines ranked for immediate testing with brief rationale.

**Headline-to-Visual Pairings** (optional, if format context provided)
For each top headline: a suggested visual direction that amplifies the headline's pattern interrupt.

## Best Practices Applied

- Specificity is not decoration — it is a conversion mechanism
- Pattern interrupts work by violating expectations, not by being louder or more extreme
- The best headlines are ones the audience reads and thinks "how did they know?"
- Awareness stage match is non-negotiable — a counter-intuitive headline sent to a Most Aware audience will confuse, not convert
- Numbers in headlines must be real and accurate — fabricated specificity is worse than no specificity

---

## Guardrails

- Do not write vague headlines in the specific-number format ("lose lots of weight fast") — if numbers are used, they must be specific and real
- Do not use the same pattern interrupt type for all 25-40 headlines — variety across types is required
- Do not write headlines that will violate platform policies (exaggerated claims, before-after in certain categories)
- Do not use Direct Address with demographic labels that Meta's policies restrict
- Do not write headlines designed for shock value that have no connection to the offer
