# Meta Format Concept Engine

**Category:** concept-systems
**Tier:** pro

## Purpose

This Skill generates a structured library of ad concepts organized across the 17 proven Meta creative format categories. Rather than generating random ad ideas, it produces batches of format-specific concepts designed for category-level testing — so teams can determine which format category works for their audience and offer, then optimize within it. Each concept includes a hook, body copy in direct-response structure, visual direction, and test recommendation.

---

## When to Use

- You need to build a concept library for a new brand, campaign, or test round
- You want to run format-category testing (testing testimonial format vs. listicle format, not just individual ads)
- You're briefing a creative team and need structured concept inputs
- You want 100+ concepts across all 17 formats in one shot
- You've identified a winning format category and want to expand the concept library within it
- You're running a new product launch and need to rapidly build a diverse concept set

---

## Inputs Required

- Brand brief or product description (use Brand Brief Template for best results)
- Target audience description
- Primary offer and key benefits
- Top 2-3 audience objections
- Proof elements available (testimonials, stats, certifications, results)
- Which format categories to prioritize (or "all 17")
- Number of concepts per format (default: 6-8 per format)
- Awareness stage of target audience

---

## What This Skill Does

Generates a full concept library organized by the 17 Meta format categories, with each concept containing a title, awareness stage, hook, direct-response body copy, visual direction, and test recommendation. Minimum output: 100 concepts across all 17 formats.

## The 17 Meta Creative Format Framework

### Format 1: Founder Story
**What it is:** A first-person narrative from the founder or a relatable authority figure about why the product exists.
**Why it works:** Creates personal trust and brand differentiation in commoditized categories. Audiences buy from people they trust before they buy products they need.
**When to use:** Cold traffic, brand awareness phases, trust-building campaigns, high-consideration purchases.
**Best for:** Products with a strong origin story, mission-driven brands, health/wellness, financial products.
**Hook types:** Personal revelation, turning point story, "I created this because," vulnerability-first.
**Proof types:** Founder credentials, before-after personal story, "why I still use it" proof.

### Format 2: Problem-Solution
**What it is:** Identifies the audience's specific pain, agitates it, then presents the product as the resolution.
**Why it works:** Mirrors the audience's existing thought pattern — they already know they have a problem. This format enters the conversation already in progress.
**When to use:** Problem Aware audiences. Any campaign where the pain point is well-defined and widely shared.
**Best for:** SaaS, productivity tools, health products, home services, anything solving a frustration.
**Hook types:** Problem statement, "if you [have this problem]," pain amplification, consequence statement.
**Proof types:** Before-after result, specific outcome metric, testimonial highlighting problem resolution.

### Format 3: Before-After
**What it is:** Shows or describes the transformation — what life was like before the product and what it's like after.
**Why it works:** Demonstrates the outcome concretely. The gap between before and after is the value proposition made visual and emotional.
**When to use:** Any product or service with a demonstrable transformation. Works for Solution Aware to Product Aware audiences.
**Best for:** Fitness, skincare, home improvement, software, anything with visible before/after.
**Hook types:** State contrast ("6 months ago I was..."), transformation reveal, specific change metric.
**Proof types:** Real photos, screenshots, specific measurements, timestamped evidence.

### Format 4: Testimonial
**What it is:** A structured customer story in the customer's voice (written or video), following the DR testimonial arc.
**Why it works:** Third-party proof from a relatable source eliminates the credibility gap. The audience trusts other customers more than they trust brands.
**When to use:** Retargeting, Product Aware audiences, high-consideration purchases, categories with skepticism.
**Best for:** Any product or service with satisfied customers who've had specific, measurable results.
**Hook types:** Result-first ("I lost 22 lbs using this"), quote drop, specific transformation opening.
**Proof types:** The testimonial itself — outcome + timeframe + unexpected benefit + credibility signal.

### Format 5: UGC Demo
**What it is:** An authentic-looking first-person video of someone using the product in real life, demonstrating how it works and their reaction.
**Why it works:** Reduces the imagination gap. Seeing someone exactly like the target audience using the product makes adoption feel achievable and natural.
**When to use:** Cold and warm traffic. High-consideration products where people want to see it in action.
**Best for:** Consumer products, tech accessories, beauty, food, fitness equipment, subscription boxes.
**Hook types:** In-use action hook, reaction hook ("I can't believe this actually works"), "let me show you."
**Proof types:** Real-time demonstration, reaction authenticity, incidental proof (messy kitchen, real environment).

### Format 6: Listicle
**What it is:** A numbered or bulleted format that delivers multiple reasons, benefits, or truths in a scannable structure.
**Why it works:** Sets clear expectations ("5 reasons") that lower the cognitive barrier to engagement. The list format signals that information is organized and easily digestible.
**When to use:** Cold traffic, content-heavy categories, complex products with multiple benefits, educational angles.
**Best for:** SaaS, financial products, health supplements with multi-benefit claims, service businesses.
**Hook types:** Number-led ("7 things [audience] wish they knew"), "reasons why," "signs that."
**Proof types:** One proof element per list item (stat, testimonial line, claim).

### Format 7: Myth-Buster
**What it is:** Identifies a false belief the target audience holds and systematically replaces it with the truth — positioning the product as the beneficiary of the true version of reality.
**Why it works:** Pattern interrupt. The audience has heard the myth so many times that contradicting it creates immediate attention and curiosity.
**When to use:** Categories with strong false beliefs or misconceptions. Particularly powerful in health, finance, fitness, education.
**Best for:** Products that work differently from what the audience expects, or that solve a problem the audience doesn't know can be solved.
**Hook types:** "The real reason [X isn't working]," "nobody told you this about [category]," "what [industry] doesn't want you to know."
**Proof types:** Research-backed counter-claim, expert authority, before-after that disproves the myth.

### Format 8: Comparison
**What it is:** Directly or implicitly contrasts the product against a competitor, alternative, or old way of doing things.
**Why it works:** Collapses the decision-making process by doing the comparison the audience was going to do anyway — on your terms.
**When to use:** Solution Aware to Product Aware audiences. Competitive markets. Category disruption positioning.
**Best for:** Software, services, subscription products, anything with clear incumbent competitors.
**Hook types:** "[Brand] vs. [competitor]," "I switched from X to Y," "why I stopped using X," feature comparison tables.
**Proof types:** Side-by-side comparison, migration story testimonial, cost difference calculation.

### Format 9: Objection Crusher
**What it is:** Leads with the audience's most powerful objection and systematically dismantles it before presenting the offer.
**Why it works:** Demonstrates deep audience understanding and establishes trust before the pitch. Objections ignored in the ad are objections raised at the decision point.
**When to use:** Skeptical audiences, retargeting, high-ticket offers, categories with strong objection patterns.
**Best for:** Programs, courses, supplements, SaaS, anything where "too expensive," "I've tried this before," or "this won't work for me" are common objections.
**Hook types:** State the objection verbatim ("If you think this is too expensive, read this"), empathy opener, "I know what you're thinking."
**Proof types:** Testimonial that addresses the specific objection, risk-reversal, guarantee.

### Format 10: Stat-Led Proof
**What it is:** Opens with a powerful, specific statistic or data point that creates immediate credibility or activates a problem-recognition moment.
**Why it works:** Data is a pattern interrupt in a sea of opinion. A specific number signals authority, research, and seriousness.
**When to use:** Problem Aware to Solution Aware audiences. B2B, health, financial, and technical categories.
**Best for:** Products solving statistically significant problems, research-backed products, SaaS, financial services.
**Hook types:** The statistic as opening line ("73% of [audience] are [experiencing problem] right now"), surprising data reveal.
**Proof types:** Source citation, additional supporting stats, expert validation of the data.

### Format 11: Process Breakdown
**What it is:** Walks the audience through how the product works step-by-step, making the mechanism understandable and believable.
**Why it works:** Mechanism credibility is one of the most underutilized conversion levers. When people understand HOW something works, they believe it can work for them.
**When to use:** Products with complex or novel mechanisms, health products, software workflows, financial strategies.
**Best for:** SaaS, health supplements with a mechanism story, service businesses with a proprietary process.
**Hook types:** "Here's exactly how [product] works in 3 steps," "the process no one explains," step-reveal format.
**Proof types:** Before/after result framed as the output of the process, testimonial referencing the process specifically.

### Format 12: Product Explainer
**What it is:** A focused demonstration and explanation of the product's core feature(s) translated directly into benefits.
**Why it works:** Feature-benefit translation bridges the gap between what a product does and why the audience should care. Pure feature lists don't convert — translated benefits do.
**When to use:** New products, product launches, complex products, technical products where the value isn't obvious.
**Best for:** Tech products, SaaS, new consumer products, anything that requires understanding to want.
**Hook types:** "What [product] actually does," demo-first reveal, "I need to show you something."
**Proof types:** Live screen recording, demonstration, specific output example.

### Format 13: Review Montage
**What it is:** Aggregates multiple short customer reviews, testimonials, or reactions into a single creative, creating a wave of social proof.
**Why it works:** Volume of proof is sometimes more persuasive than a single, detailed testimonial. The pattern of multiple voices saying the same thing activates social consensus.
**When to use:** Products with a large review base, retargeting, warm traffic. Particularly powerful for ecommerce.
**Best for:** Consumer products with Amazon/Trustpilot reviews, supplements, SaaS with G2 reviews.
**Hook types:** "See what [number] customers are saying," review excerpt as hook, "I had to share these."
**Proof types:** Screenshot of actual reviews, star ratings, verbatim customer language.

### Format 14: Offer-Led Promo
**What it is:** Leads with the deal, discount, or time-sensitive offer as the primary creative driver.
**Why it works:** For Product Aware and Most Aware audiences, the offer is the decision trigger. They already know the product — they need a reason to act now.
**When to use:** Retargeting, promotional campaigns, Black Friday/seasonal, existing customer offers, last-chance campaigns.
**Best for:** Ecommerce, subscription products, courses, software with trial-to-paid conversion sequences.
**Hook types:** Deal-first ("Save 40% this week only"), countdown-style, "your last chance" urgency.
**Proof types:** Value stack (show total value vs. deal price), testimonial affirming the value of the price.

### Format 15: Day-in-the-Life
**What it is:** Shows or describes how the product fits naturally into the daily routine of someone exactly like the target audience.
**Why it works:** Lifestyle integration makes adoption feel effortless and identity-consistent. The audience sees themselves in the scenario and can imagine owning and using the product.
**When to use:** Warm traffic, aspirational consumer products, health and wellness, productivity tools.
**Best for:** Consumer products, health/beauty, home products, any product where the lifestyle context matters.
**Hook types:** Morning routine reveal, "a day using [product]," time-stamped day narrative.
**Proof types:** Real footage or authentic recreation, before-day vs. after-day contrast.

### Format 16: FAQ Style
**What it is:** Structures the creative as answers to the most common questions the audience has about the product or offer.
**Why it works:** Mirrors the way people naturally research products. If your ad answers the exact questions they're about to Google, you've pre-empted the research phase.
**When to use:** Solution Aware to Product Aware audiences. High-consideration purchases where information-gathering is part of the journey.
**Best for:** High-ticket products, B2B, health/financial products with compliance concerns, technical products.
**Hook types:** Question-led opening, "I get asked [question] all the time," "The top 5 questions about [product]."
**Proof types:** The answer itself — authoritative, specific, with evidence where needed.

### Format 17: Mistake-Driven Angle
**What it is:** Opens with a common mistake the target audience is making and positions the product as the way to stop making it.
**Why it works:** The mistake hook is a pattern interrupt that triggers identity threat response ("Am I doing this wrong?"). It generates engagement from curiosity and from the desire to not look foolish.
**When to use:** Cold and Problem Aware audiences. Categories where conventional wisdom is wrong or outdated.
**Best for:** Fitness, investing, skincare, business/marketing, parenting, any category with evolving best practices.
**Hook types:** "Stop [doing this thing]," "The [number] mistake [audience] makes," "Why [common practice] is actually hurting you."
**Proof types:** Data showing the mistake causes the problem, before-after from someone who stopped making the mistake.

## Concept Generation Workflow

1. **Ingest brand brief and offer details**
2. **Select target formats** (all 17 or specified subset)
3. **Generate concepts per format** using the structure below
4. **Organize by format** in the output
5. **Flag top concepts per format** for immediate testing

---

## Concept Output Structure

For each concept:
```
CONCEPT TITLE: [Descriptive name]
FORMAT: [One of the 17]
AWARENESS STAGE: [Unaware / Problem Aware / Solution Aware / Product Aware / Most Aware]
ANGLE CATEGORY: [Emotional angle: frustration / aspiration / fear / skepticism / curiosity]

HOOK/HEADLINE: [Specific, numbered where applicable, pattern interrupt where appropriate]

BODY COPY:
  HOOK: [Opening line — activates emotion and stops the scroll]
  AGITATE: [Deepens the pain or desire — 2-3 sentences]
  PROVE: [Specific proof — testimonial, stat, result, or mechanism — 2-3 sentences]
  CTA: [Friction-reducing action instruction — 1-2 sentences]

VISUAL DIRECTION: [Specific enough for a designer or AI image tool — talent, environment, action, style, mood]

TEST RECOMMENDATION: [What to test this concept against and which variable to watch]
```

---

## Output Requirements

Minimum 100 concepts across all 17 formats (6-8 per format), following the exact concept output structure above. Organized by format category with a header for each format.

---

## Best Practices Applied

- Format-category testing is more strategically valuable than individual concept testing
- Concepts are grounded in brand brief inputs, not invented generically
- Each concept is complete enough to brief a designer or copywriter without additional clarification
- Visual directions are specific (talent type, setting, action, mood) not generic ("engaging visual")
- Hooks use numbers and specificity as defaults, not as decorative elements

---

## Guardrails

- Do not generate generic concepts that could apply to any brand — all concepts must use specific details from the brand brief
- Do not use the same hook formula more than twice per format category
- Do not write visual directions that are too vague to execute ("nice lifestyle photo")
- Do not produce concepts without awareness stage classification
- Do not skip the test recommendation — every concept must have a stated test partner
