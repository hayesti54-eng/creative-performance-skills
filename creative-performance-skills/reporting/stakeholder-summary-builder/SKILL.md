# Stakeholder Summary Builder

**Category:** reporting
**Tier:** agency

## Purpose

This Skill takes a detailed creative performance report or raw data and converts it into a concise, non-technical stakeholder summary designed for executives, clients, or investors who don't need the full data — they need to know what's happening, whether the program is working, and what decisions need to be made. This summary is a communication tool, not an analysis document. It is optimized for speed of comprehension and confidence in the team managing the creative program.

---

## When to Use

- A client, executive, or investor wants an update on the creative program without reading a full report
- You need to present creative performance in a leadership meeting or QBR
- A founder is reviewing the paid media program and needs a 2-minute briefing
- You want to send a weekly or monthly update to stakeholders who don't live in the ads manager
- You need to request budget or approval for a next round of creative investment
- You're presenting test results and want the "so what" version for decision-makers

---

## Inputs Required

- Creative performance report or raw data for the period
- Stakeholder context: who are they? what do they care about? (revenue growth, ROAS, efficiency, testing velocity)
- Primary business goal this creative program is tied to
- Any decisions or approvals that need to come from the stakeholder
- Budget context (are we on track? over? under?)

---

## What This Skill Does

Compresses a full performance report into a structured 1-page (or sub-500-word) stakeholder summary with: headline metric status, program health signal, top creative story, key decision required, and next steps.

---

## Analysis Workflow

1. **Identify what the stakeholder cares about most**
   Revenue, ROAS, CPL, efficiency, testing velocity, or creative output volume. Lead with that metric.

2. **Write the headline status (one sentence)**
   "Creative performance is [on track / above target / below target] this [period], with [primary metric] at [value] vs. [benchmark/goal]."

3. **Write the program health signal**
   Green, Yellow, or Red:
   - Green: Primary metric at or above target, pipeline of new creative in testing
   - Yellow: Primary metric within 15% of target, one or two creatives in decline, replacements in motion
   - Red: Primary metric below target by >15%, multiple creatives declining, no confirmed winner in rotation

4. **Write the top creative story**
   What is the most interesting or important creative development this period? Frame as a narrative, not a data point: "We discovered that leads with a specific customer outcome in the first 3 seconds are outperforming our previous benefit-led hooks by 2.3x. We're rolling out 4 new variations of this approach."

5. **Write the key decision required**
   If the stakeholder needs to approve anything, decide anything, or be aware of a risk, state it plainly: "We need approval to increase testing budget by $3,000 this month to run 3 new concept variations before the Q2 campaign window closes."

6. **Write next steps (3 bullets max)**
   What happens next, with dates: "Kill 3 underperforming ads by Friday. Launch 4 new hook variations next Monday. Review results at the end of the month for scale decisions."

7. **Produce the final summary**
   Assemble all elements. Total length: under 500 words. No jargon. No raw data tables (these belong in the appendix of the full report, not the summary).

---

## Output Requirements

**Stakeholder Summary**

```
CREATIVE PROGRAM UPDATE: [Month / Period]

STATUS: 🟢 GREEN / 🟡 YELLOW / 🔴 RED

[Primary metric]: [Value] vs. [Target] — [trend direction]

WHAT'S WORKING
[1-2 sentences: specific creative direction that is performing and why it matters]

WHAT WE'RE ADDRESSING
[1-2 sentences: what is underperforming and what specific action we're taking]

THE CREATIVE STORY THIS PERIOD
[2-3 sentences: the most important creative insight or development, framed as a narrative]

DECISION NEEDED (if applicable)
[One specific question or approval request]

NEXT STEPS
• [Action + date]
• [Action + date]
• [Action + date]
```

---

## Best Practices Applied

- Stakeholder summaries lead with the metric the stakeholder cares about, not the metric the operator cares about
- Program health signals (Green/Yellow/Red) allow rapid assessment without reading the data
- "The Creative Story" section makes the program feel intelligent and strategic, not just transactional
- Decisions required are isolated so they don't get buried in context
- Three next steps, not ten — stakeholders don't need operational detail, they need confidence in the direction

---

## Guardrails

- Do not include raw data tables — summary only, data belongs in the full report
- Do not use technical jargon (CPC, CPM, lookalike, holdout) without plain-language context
- Do not bury a decision request in a paragraph — give it its own labeled section
- Do not overclaim on creative performance — if results are mixed, say so clearly
- Do not write a summary that requires the stakeholder to ask follow-up questions about the status

---

## Example Requests

1. "Here's our full monthly creative report. Write me a 1-page stakeholder summary I can send to our founder."

2. "We're presenting to the client in a leadership meeting tomorrow. Take this data and write the 3-minute verbal summary I'll open with, plus a written version to share on screen."

3. "Write a Green/Yellow/Red status update for our creative program this week. We're on track with ROAS but one of our top creatives is showing fatigue and we're briefing replacements."
